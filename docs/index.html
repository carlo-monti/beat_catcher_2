BEAT CATCHER

WHAT IT IS

Beat Catcher is a beat-tracking device for synchronizing electronic systems and human musicians. Using one sensor on the kick and another on the snare, Beat Catcher detects the tempo currently held by the drummer and sends out a MIDI CLOCK signal that keeps the electronic instruments in sync.

WHAT IT DOES

Beat Catcher act as a MIDI master clock for other MIDI sync-able gear (synths, sequencers, effects, ...). However, unlike other master clock, it stays in sync with the drummer. It means that if the drummer slows down, it will slow down too!

Let's see a usage example. Let's suppose that your song starts with a drum groove and, after that, a synth sequence comes in (i.e. A-ha - Take On Me).

If you want to play the song live, the drummer must have an in-ear monitor with a metronome, otherwise the drum beat may fail to match the exact bpm of the synth! With Beat Catcher there is no need of a metronome. The drummer can start the whole system (by simply tapping on it) and the synth will receive a MIDI Clock signal that is in synch.

Everything that is connected as slave to the MIDI Clock will be perfectly on time. It could be a sequencer, an arpeggiator, a looper or even a time-based effect (like a delay or a modulator).

HOW TO USE

The setup is very simple. First, you have to attach the two sensors (that are similar to Roland RT-30) on the kick and on the snare. After that, you place Beat Catcher where you like it and connect the two wires coming from the sensors to it. Finally you connect your MIDI equipment to it using a standard MIDI cable. The system is ready! You can start it by tapping a 4/4 on it with a drumstick (alternatively you can select the exact bpm and manually trigger the start of the sequence). The system will start the MIDI Clock and you can start playing.

Remember that Beat Catcher can follow your tempo only when you are using kick and snare! If it don't detect any hit, it will keep up with the last bpm detected. You can then follow the tempo of the MIDI Clock by connecting earphones to it or by looking at the four leds that are on top of it (that indicates the 1/4 notes).
